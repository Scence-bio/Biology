🦠 Microbiology - Study of Microorganisms

Comprehensive study of bacteria, viruses, and other microorganisms

🔵
Staphylococcus aureus
Gram-Positive
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-positive rods in chains (boxcar-shaped)
Non-motile (unlike other Bacillus species)
Spore-forming: central, oval spores
Capsule: polypeptide (poly-D-glutamate)
Catalase test: Positive
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Anthrax toxin (tripartite exotoxin)
Protective antigen (PA): binds host cells
Edema factor (EF): increases cAMP → edema
Lethal factor (LF): metalloprotease → cell death
Capsule: inhibits phagocytosis
🏥 Clinical Importance
Cutaneous anthrax: black eschar with edema
Inhalational anthrax: severe respiratory distress
Gastrointestinal anthrax: bloody diarrhea
Anthrax meningitis: rare, high mortality
Used as bioterrorism agent
5
🍚
Bacillus cereus
Gram-Positive
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-positive rods with square ends
Motile via peritrichous flagella
Forms central, oval, heat-resistant spores
Catalase-positive; beta-hemolysis on blood agar
Aerobic or facultative anaerobe
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Produces emetic (vomiting) toxin - cereulide
Produces diarrheal toxin - heat-labile
Additional toxins: Hbl, Nhe, CytK
Spores resist heat, disinfectants, and drying
🏥 Clinical Importance
Emetic type food poisoning: rapid onset (1–6 hrs), linked to rice
Diarrheal type: slower onset (8–16 hrs), linked to meat/vegetables
Eye infections and wound infections
Bacteremia in immunocompromised patients
6
🍼
Listeria monocytogenes
Gram-Positive
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-positive rods in short chains or singles
Motile at room temperature (tumbling motility)
Catalase-positive, oxidase-negative
Grows at refrigeration temperatures
Weak beta-hemolysis on blood agar
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Internalins (InlA, InlB): promote cell invasion
Listeriolysin O (LLO): escapes phagolysosomes
ActA protein: induces actin polymerization
Phospholipases: aid vacuole escape
Crosses blood-brain and placental barriers
🏥 Clinical Importance
Listeriosis: fever, diarrhea, meningitis, sepsis
High risk in pregnant women and immunocompromised
Can cause miscarriage and neonatal death
Transmitted via contaminated food (cheese, deli meats)
7
🥢
Clostridium tetani
Gram-Positive (Obligate Anaerobe)
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-positive rods with terminal spherical spores (drumstick)
Obligate anaerobe
Motile (except type VI strains)
Spores highly resistant to heat and disinfectants
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Tetanospasmin: potent neurotoxin
Blocks inhibitory neurotransmitters (GABA, glycine)
Tetanolysin: hemolysin
Toxin spreads via retrograde axonal transport
🏥 Clinical Importance
Causes tetanus: muscle rigidity and spasms
Uncontrolled muscle contractions
Infection via contaminated wounds
No person-to-person transmission
Preventable by vaccination
8
🥫
Clostridium botulinum
Gram-Positive (Obligate Anaerobe)
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-positive rods with terminal spores ("tennis racket")
Obligate anaerobe
Produces heat-resistant endospores
Catalase-negative; non-motile (Group I)
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Botulinum neurotoxin (BoNT): most potent biological toxin
Blocks acetylcholine release at neuromuscular junctions
Toxin types A, B, E, and F affect humans
Zinc metalloprotease cleaves SNARE proteins
🏥 Clinical Importance
Foodborne botulism: flaccid paralysis
Infant botulism: spores from honey
Wound botulism: anaerobic wound infection
Symptoms: blurred vision, dry mouth, descending paralysis
Treated with antitoxin and supportive care
9
🔴
Escherichia coli
Gram-Negative
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-negative bacilli (pink rods on Gram stain)
Facultative anaerobe
Lactose fermenter (pink colonies on MacConkey agar)
Motile with peritrichous flagella
Oxidase-negative, catalase-positive
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Adhesins and exotoxins as key virulence factors
Pathotypes: EHEC, EPEC, ETEC, EIEC, EAEC, DAEC
Shiga toxin (EHEC): causes bloody diarrhea and HUS
Enterotoxins (ETEC): cause watery diarrhea
🏥 Clinical Importance
Most common cause of UTIs
Neonatal meningitis and sepsis
Traveler's diarrhea (ETEC) and foodborne outbreaks (EHEC)
Can lead to hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS)
Growing antibiotic resistance concern
10
🌡️
Salmonella typhi
Gram-Negative
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-negative bacillus (pink rods on Gram stain)
Motile with peritrichous flagella
Non-spore-forming and non-encapsulated
Lactose non-fermenter; H₂S producer
Grows on MacConkey and XLD agar
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Vi capsule: resists phagocytosis
Type III secretion system (T3SS)
Salmonella pathogenicity islands (SPIs)
Endotoxin (LPS): triggers inflammation and fever
Survives and replicates inside macrophages
🏥 Clinical Importance
Typhoid fever: prolonged fever, abdominal pain, rose spots
Can lead to intestinal perforation and sepsis
Transmitted via contaminated food or water
Diagnosed via blood culture or Widal test
Preventable by Vi conjugate vaccine (TCV)
11
💢
Shigella dysenteriae
Gram-Negative
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-negative bacillus (pink rods on Gram stain)
Non-motile, non-spore-forming, non-capsulated
Grows on MacConkey and SS agar
Lactose non-fermenter; does not produce H₂S
Optimum growth at 37°C, pH 7.4
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Shiga toxin: inhibits protein synthesis
Invades colonic epithelial cells via Type III secretion
Escapes phagocytic vacuoles
Induces apoptosis in macrophages
Disrupts intestinal wall integrity
🏥 Clinical Importance
Shigellosis: bloody diarrhea, fever, abdominal cramps
Low infectious dose (10–100 organisms)
Can lead to Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS)
Transmitted via fecal-oral route
Treated with hydration and antibiotics in severe cases
12
💙
Pseudomonas aeruginosa
Gram-Negative (Strict Aerobe)
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-negative bacillus (pink rods on Gram stain)
Motile with polar flagella
Strict aerobe; oxidase-positive
Produces blue-green pigment (pyocyanin)
Grows on MacConkey and cetrimide agar
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Forms biofilms resistant to antibiotics
Secretes exotoxin A (inhibits protein synthesis)
Type III secretion system for toxin injection
Produces elastase, proteases, and pyocyanin
Highly adaptable to diverse environments
🏥 Clinical Importance
Pneumonia, especially in ventilated patients
Common in burn wound and cystic fibrosis infections
UTIs, sepsis, and keratitis
Major agent of nosocomial infections
Difficult to treat due to multi-drug resistance
13
⚪
Neisseria gonorrhoeae
Gram-Negative
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-negative diplococci (kidney-shaped)
Often found inside neutrophils
Non-motile, non-spore-forming
Oxidase-positive, catalase-positive
Ferments glucose only (not maltose)
Grows on Thayer-Martin or chocolate agar with CO₂
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Pili: aid in attachment and resist phagocytosis
Opa proteins: mediate adhesion to host cells
Porin proteins: interfere with neutrophil killing
IgA protease: cleaves mucosal antibodies
Lipooligosaccharide (LOS): triggers inflammation
🏥 Clinical Importance
Urethritis, cervicitis, pharyngitis, proctitis
Can lead to pelvic inflammatory disease (PID)
Ophthalmia neonatorum in newborns
May cause disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI)
Increasing resistance to antibiotics like ceftriaxone
14
🌪️
Helicobacter pylori
Gram-Negative (Microaerophilic)
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-negative, spiral-shaped rods
Motile with 4–6 polar sheathed flagella
Microaerophilic (requires low oxygen)
Urease-positive (neutralizes stomach acid)
Grows on Skirrow's or Campy agar
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Urease: produces ammonia to buffer stomach acid
Flagella: corkscrew motility through mucus layer
Adhesins: bind to gastric epithelium (BabA, SabA)
VacA toxin: induces cell vacuolation and apoptosis
CagA protein: disrupts cell signaling
🏥 Clinical Importance
Causes chronic gastritis and peptic ulcers
Linked to gastric adenocarcinoma and MALT lymphoma
Transmitted via oral-oral or fecal-oral routes
Diagnosed by urea breath test, stool antigen, or biopsy
Treated with triple therapy: PPI + antibiotics
15
🫁
Streptococcus pneumoniae
Gram-Positive
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-positive, lancet-shaped diplococci
Alpha-hemolytic on blood agar (green zone)
Catalase-negative; optochin-sensitive
Bile soluble (dissolves in bile salts)
Encapsulated with >80 serotypes
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Polysaccharide capsule: major virulence factor
Pneumolysin: damages host cells
IgA protease: cleaves mucosal antibodies
Teichoic acids: promote inflammation and adherence
Autolysins: release toxins and cell wall fragments
🏥 Clinical Importance
Community-acquired pneumonia (rusty sputum)
Major cause of meningitis in children and elderly
Otitis media, sinusitis, and bronchitis
Can cause bacteremia and endocarditis
Preventable by pneumococcal vaccines (PCV13, PPSV23)
16
👶
Haemophilus influenzae
Gram-Negative
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-negative pleomorphic coccobacilli
Requires factors X (hemin) and V (NAD⁺) for growth
Grows on chocolate agar, not on blood agar
Non-motile, non-spore🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-positive cocci in clusters (purple under Gram stain)
Catalase test: positive
Coagulase test: positive
Grows on Mannitol Salt Agar and ferments mannitol
Shows beta-hemolysis (clear zone) on blood agar
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Coagulase (clots plasma)
Protein A (binds antibodies to evade immune system)
Toxins: TSST-1 and enterotoxins
Major concern for antibiotic resistance (MRSA strains)
🏥 Clinical Importance
Skin infections: boils, abscesses, impetigo
Respiratory infections: pneumonia
Systemic infections: sepsis, osteomyelitis, endocarditis
Toxin-related illnesses: food poisoning, toxic shock syndrome

🔗
Streptococcus pyogenes
Gram-Positive
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-positive cocci in chains
Catalase test: Negative
Beta-hemolytic on blood agar
Group A Streptococcus (GAS)
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
M protein: resists phagocytosis
Streptolysins O and S: lyse red blood cells
Exotoxins: cause toxic shock and scarlet fever
Spread by respiratory droplets or contact
🏥 Clinical Importance
Strep throat (pharyngitis) and impetigo
Scarlet fever
Rheumatic fever
Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis
Necrotizing fasciitis ("flesh-eating disease")
3
⚪
Enterococcus faecium
Gram-Positive
🔬 Identification & Characteristics
Gram-positive cocci in pairs or short chains
Catalase test: Negative
Gamma-hemolytic or alpha-hemolytic on blood agar
Grows in 6.5% NaCl and bile-esculin agar
Intrinsically resistant to many antibiotics
⚔️ Virulence & Pathogenicity
Adhesins: facilitate attachment to host tissues
Cytolysin: can lyse red blood cells
Ability to form biofilms on medical devices
Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus (VRE) strains
🏥 Clinical Importance
Urinary tract infections (UTIs)
Bacteremia and endocarditis in immunocompromised patients
Intra-abdominal and wound infections
Major concern for antibiotic-resistant infections
