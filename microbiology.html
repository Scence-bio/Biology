Examination of bacterial structure, pathogenicity and clinical significance

Bacteria 1
Staphylococcus aureus
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-positive cocci in clusters (purple under Gram stain)
Catalase test: positive
Coagulase test: positive
Grows on Mannitol Salt Agar and ferments mannitol (turns medium yellow)
Shows beta-hemolysis (clear zone) on blood agar
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Found naturally on the skin, in the nose, and mucous membranes
  • Coagulase (clots plasma)
  • Protein A (binds antibodies to evade immune system)
  • Toxins: TSST-1 and enterotoxins (cause toxic shock and food poisoning)
  • Major concern for antibiotic resistance, especially MRSA strains
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Skin infections like boils, abscesses, and impetigo
  • Respiratory infections, such as pneumonia
  • Systemic infections: sepsis, osteomyelitis, and endocarditis
  • Toxin-related illnesses: food poisoning and toxic shock syndrome
Bacteria 2
Streptococcus pyogenes
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-positive cocci in chains (purple spheres in lines)
Catalase test: Negative (helps distinguish from staphylococci)
Beta-hemolytic on blood agar (clear zone around colonies)
Group A Streptococcus (GAS), based on Lancefield classification
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • M protein: resists phagocytosis
  • Streptolysins O and S: lyse red blood cells
  • Exotoxins: cause toxic shock and scarlet fever
  • Spread by respiratory droplets or contact with infected wounds
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Common infections: strep throat (pharyngitis) and impetigo
  • Scarlet fever
  • Rheumatic fever
  • Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis
  • Necrotizing fasciitis ("flesh-eating disease")
  • Toxic shock-like syndrome
Bacteria 3
Enterococcus faecium
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-positive cocci in pairs or short chains
Catalase test: Negative
Gamma-hemolytic or alpha-hemolytic on blood agar
Grows in 6.5% NaCl and bile-esculin agar (blackening)
Can survive harsh conditions including high salt and temperature
Intrinsically resistant to many antibiotics
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Adhesins: facilitate attachment to host tissues
  • Cytolysin: can lyse red blood cells and other cell types
  • Ability to form biofilms on medical devices
  • Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus (VRE) is a major clinical concern
  • Spread primarily via direct contact in healthcare settings
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Causes urinary tract infections (UTIs)
  • Bacteremia and endocarditis in immunocompromised patients
  • Intra-abdominal and wound infections
  • Associated with antibiotic-resistant infections
Bacteria 4
Bacillus anthracis
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-positive rods in chains (purple boxcar-shaped rods)
Non-motile (unlike other Bacillus species)
Spore-forming: central, oval spores that do not bulge the cell
Capsule: polypeptide (poly-D-glutamate), not polysaccharide
Aerobic growth; non-hemolytic colonies on blood agar
Catalase test: Positive
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Anthrax toxin (tripartite exotoxin): PA, EF, LF
  • Protective antigen (PA): binds host cells, facilitates entry
  • Edema factor (EF): increases cAMP ‚Üí edema
  • Lethal factor (LF): metalloprotease ‚Üí cell death
  • Capsule: inhibits phagocytosis
  • Transmission via spores‚Äîthrough inhalation, ingestion, or skin contact
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Cutaneous anthrax: black eschar with surrounding edema (most common form)
  • Inhalational anthrax: severe respiratory distress, often fatal
  • Gastrointestinal anthrax: abdominal pain, vomiting, bloody diarrhea
  • Anthrax meningitis: rare, high mortality
  • Used as a bioterrorism agent (e.g., 2001 US postal attacks)
Bacteria 5
Bacillus cereus
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-positive rods with square ends
Motile via peritrichous flagella
Forms central, oval, heat-resistant spores
Aerobic or facultative anaerobe
Catalase-positive; shows beta-hemolysis on blood agar
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Produces emetic (vomiting) and diarrheal toxins
  • Emetic toxin (cereulide) is heat-stable
  • Diarrheal toxin is heat-labile
  • Additional toxins: Hbl, Nhe, CytK
  • Spores resist heat, disinfectants, and drying
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Causes two types of food poisoning
  • Emetic type: rapid onset (1‚Äì6 hrs), linked to rice
  • Diarrheal type: slower onset (8‚Äì16 hrs), linked to meat/veg
  • Can cause eye infections, wound infections, and bacteremia
  • Found in contaminated hospital equipment and products
Bacteria 6
Listeria monocytogenes
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-positive rods, often in short chains or singles
Motile at room temperature (tumbling motility)
Facultative anaerobe; catalase-positive, oxidase-negative
Grows on blood agar; weak beta-hemolysis
Can grow at refrigeration temperatures (psychrotrophic)
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Internalins (InlA, InlB): promote epithelial cell invasion
  • Listeriolysin O (LLO): allows escape from phagolysosomes
  • ActA protein: induces actin polymerization for cell-to-cell spread
  • Phospholipases (PlcA, PlcB): aid vacuole escape
  • Evades immune system by intracellular movement
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Causes listeriosis: fever, diarrhea, meningitis, sepsis
  • High risk in pregnant women, newborns, elderly, immunocompromised
  • Can lead to miscarriage, neonatal death, or neurolisteriosis
  • Transmitted via contaminated food (e.g., cheese, deli meats)
  • Treated with ampicillin ¬± gentamicin
Bacteria 7
Clostridium tetani
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-positive rods with terminal spherical spores (drumstick appearance)
Obligate anaerobe; grows in oxygen-free environments
Motile (except type VI strains); has flagella
Spores are highly resistant to heat and disinfectants
Grows on blood agar; enhanced by serum or reducing agents
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Produces two toxins: tetanospasmin (neurotoxin) and tetanolysin
  • Tetanospasmin blocks inhibitory neurotransmitters (GABA, glycine)
  • Causes uncontrolled muscle contraction and rigidity
  • Toxin spreads via retrograde axonal transport to CNS
  • No person-to-person transmission; infection via contaminated wounds
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Causes tetanus: muscle spasms, lockjaw, opisthotonus
  • Infection follows contamination of wounds with spores from soil
  • Preventable by tetanus vaccination (tetanus toxoid)
  • Treatment includes antitoxin and supportive care
Bacteria 8
Clostridium botulinum
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-positive rods with terminal spores (bulging, "tennis racket" shape)
Obligate anaerobe; grows in oxygen-free environments
Produces heat-resistant endospores
Slow-growing on enriched anaerobic media
Catalase-negative; non-motile (Group I)
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Botulinum neurotoxin (BoNT): blocks acetylcholine release
  • Toxin types A, B, E, and F affect humans
  • BoNT is a zinc metalloprotease that cleaves SNARE proteins
  • Spores germinate in anaerobic conditions (canned food, wounds)
  • Toxin preformed in foodborne; produced in vivo in infant/wound botulism
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Foodborne botulism: ingestion of preformed toxin ‚Üí flaccid paralysis
  • Infant botulism: spores germinate in gut (e.g., from honey)
  • Wound botulism: infection in anaerobic wound sites
  • Symptoms: blurred vision, dry mouth, descending paralysis
  • Treated with antitoxin and supportive care
Bacteria 9
Escherichia coli
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-negative bacilli (pink rods on Gram stain)
Facultative anaerobe (grows with or without oxygen)
Lactose fermenter (pink colonies on MacConkey agar)
Motile with peritrichous flagella (except some strains)
Oxidase-negative, catalase-positive
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Possesses adhesins and exotoxins as key virulence factors
  • Pathotypes: EHEC, EPEC, ETEC, EIEC, EAEC, DAEC
  • Shiga toxin (EHEC) causes bloody diarrhea and HUS
  • Enterotoxins (ETEC) cause watery diarrhea
  • Acquires virulence via plasmids, phages, pathogenicity islands
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Most common cause of urinary tract infections (UTIs)
  • Causes neonatal meningitis and sepsis
  • Traveler's diarrhea (ETEC) and foodborne outbreaks (EHEC)
  • Can lead to hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS)
  • Antibiotic resistance is a growing concern globally
Bacteria 10
Salmonella typhi
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-negative bacillus (pink rods on Gram stain)
Motile with peritrichous flagella
Non-spore-forming and non-encapsulated
Facultative anaerobe; grows on MacConkey and XLD agar
Lactose non-fermenter; H‚ÇÇS producer
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Vi capsule: resists phagocytosis and immune clearance
  • Type III secretion system (T3SS): injects effector proteins
  • Salmonella pathogenicity islands (SPIs): encode invasion genes
  • Endotoxin (LPS): triggers inflammation and fever
  • Survives and replicates inside macrophages
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Causes typhoid fever: prolonged fever, abdominal pain, rose spots
  • Can lead to intestinal perforation, sepsis, encephalopathy
  • Transmitted via contaminated food or water
  • Diagnosed via blood culture or Widal test
  • Preventable by Vi conjugate vaccine (TCV)
Bacteria 11
Shigella dysenteriae
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-negative bacillus (pink rods on Gram stain)
Non-motile, non-spore-forming, non-capsulated
Facultative anaerobe; grows on MacConkey and SS agar
Lactose non-fermenter; does not produce H‚ÇÇS

ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Produces Shiga toxin: inhibits protein synthesis, causes cell death
  • Invades colonic epithelial cells via Type III secretion system
  • Escapes phagocytic vacuoles and spreads cell-to-cell
  • Induces apoptosis in macrophages, releasing IL-1Œ≤
  • Disrupts intestinal wall integrity, leading to ulceration
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Causes shigellosis: bloody diarrhea, fever, abdominal cramps
  • Low infectious dose (10‚Äì100 organisms)
  • Can lead to Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS)
  • Transmitted via fecal-oral route (food, water, flies)
  • Treated with hydration and antibiotics in severe cases
Bacteria 12
Pseudomonas aeruginosa
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-negative bacillus (pink rods on Gram stain)
Motile with polar flagella
Strict aerobe; oxidase-positive
Produces blue-green pigment (pyocyanin)
Grows on MacConkey and cetrimide agar
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Forms biofilms that resist antibiotics and immune attack
  • Secretes exotoxin A (inhibits protein synthesis)
  • Has Type III secretion system for injecting toxins
  • Produces elastase, proteases, and pyocyanin
  • Highly adaptable to diverse environments
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Causes pneumonia, especially in ventilated patients
  • Common in burn wound and cystic fibrosis infections
  • Leads to UTIs, sepsis, and keratitis
  • Major agent of nosocomial infections
  • Difficult to treat due to multi-drug resistance
Bacteria 13
Neisseria gonorrhoeae
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-negative diplococci (kidney-shaped, often inside neutrophils)
Non-motile, non-spore-forming
Oxidase-positive, catalase-positive
Ferments glucose only (not maltose)
Grows on Thayer-Martin or chocolate agar with CO‚ÇÇ
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Pili: aid in attachment and resist phagocytosis
  • Opa proteins: mediate adhesion to host cells
  • Porin proteins: interfere with neutrophil killing
  • IgA protease: cleaves mucosal antibodies
  • Lipooligosaccharide (LOS): triggers inflammation and tissue damage
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Causes urethritis, cervicitis, pharyngitis, and proctitis
  • Can lead to pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) and infertility
  • Ophthalmia neonatorum in newborns
  • May cause disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI)
  • Increasing resistance to antibiotics like ceftriaxone
Bacteria 14
Helicobacter pylori
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-negative, spiral-shaped rods
Motile with 4‚Äì6 polar sheathed flagella
Microaerophilic (requires low oxygen)
Urease-positive (neutralizes stomach acid)
Grows on selective media like Skirrow's or Campy agar
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Urease: produces ammonia to buffer stomach acid
  • Flagella: corkscrew motility through mucus layer
  • Adhesins: bind to gastric epithelium (e.g., BabA, SabA)
  • VacA toxin: induces cell vacuolation and apoptosis
  • CagA protein: disrupts cell signaling via type IV secretion system
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Causes chronic gastritis and peptic ulcers
  • Linked to gastric adenocarcinoma and MALT lymphoma
  • Transmitted via oral-oral or fecal-oral routes
  • Diagnosed by urea breath test, stool antigen, or biopsy
  • Treated with triple therapy: PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin
Bacteria 15
Streptococcus pneumoniae
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-positive, lancet-shaped diplococci or short chains
Alpha-hemolytic on blood agar (green zone)
Catalase-negative; optochin-sensitive
Bile soluble (dissolves in bile salts)
Encapsulated with >80 serotypes
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Polysaccharide capsule: antiphagocytic, major virulence factor
  • Pneumolysin: damages host cells and inhibits immune response
  • IgA protease: cleaves mucosal antibodies
  • Teichoic acids: promote inflammation and adherence
  • Autolysins: release toxins and cell wall fragments
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Causes community-acquired pneumonia (rusty sputum)
  • Major cause of meningitis, especially in children and elderly
  • Leads to otitis media, sinusitis, and bronchitis
  • Can cause bacteremia and endocarditis
  • Preventable by pneumococcal vaccines (PCV13, PPSV23)
Bacteria 16
Haemophilus influenzae
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-negative pleomorphic coccobacilli (pink, short rods)
Requires factors X (hemin) and V (NAD‚Å∫) for growth
Grows on chocolate agar, not on blood agar
Non-motile, non-spore-forming
Type b (Hib) is encapsulated; others may lack capsule
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Capsule (Hib): polyribosyl ribitol phosphate (PRP), antiphagocytic
  • Adhesins (HMW1, HMW2): promote epithelial attachment
  • Lipooligosaccharide (LOS): triggers inflammation and ciliary damage
  • IgA1 protease: cleaves mucosal antibodies
  • Outer membrane proteins: aid in immune evasion
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Causes meningitis, epiglottitis, pneumonia, and otitis media
  • Non-typeable strains cause sinusitis, bronchitis, conjunctivitis
  • Transmitted via respiratory droplets
  • Hib vaccine has dramatically reduced invasive disease
  • Treated with antibiotics; resistance is emerging
Bacteria 17
Nocardia
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-positive, branching filamentous rods
Weakly acid-fast (Ziehl-Neelsen stain)
Aerobic; slow-growing on standard media
Forms chalky, white colonies with aerial hyphae
Resembles fungi microscopically but is bacterial
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Survives inside macrophages by blocking phagosome-lysosome fusion
  • Produces catalase and superoxide dismutase (resist oxidative killing)
  • Mycolic acids in cell wall aid acid-fastness and immune evasion
  • Can disseminate to CNS and other organs
  • Capable of relapse despite treatment
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Pulmonary nocardiosis: mimics TB or fungal infections
  • Cutaneous nocardiosis: cellulitis, abscesses, mycetoma
  • CNS involvement: brain abscesses in immunocompromised
  • Acquired via inhalation or traumatic skin inoculation
  • Treated with sulfonamides (TMP-SMX); prolonged therapy required
Bacteria 18
Yersinia pestis
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-negative coccobacilli with bipolar ("safety pin") staining
Non-motile, non-spore-forming, facultative anaerobe
Grows on ordinary media; optimal at 27¬∞C
Shows pleomorphism in older cultures
Catalase-positive; oxidase-negative
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • F1 capsule antigen: inhibits phagocytosis
  • Type III secretion system (T3SS): injects Yops into host cells
  • Endotoxin (LPS): causes systemic inflammation
  • Murine toxin: affects vascular system and liver
  • V and W antigens: block intracellular killing
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Bubonic plague: swollen lymph nodes (buboes), fever
  • Septicemic plague: bloodstream infection, shock
  • Pneumonic plague: respiratory transmission, highly fatal
  • Transmitted via fleas, direct contact, or aerosols
  • Treated with antibiotics (streptomycin, doxycycline)
Bacteria 19
Vibrio cholerae
üî¨ Identification & Characteristics:
Gram-negative, comma-shaped bacilli (pink on Gram stain)
Highly motile with a single polar flagellum (darting motility)
Oxidase-positive, catalase-positive
Grows on alkaline media; yellow colonies on TCBS agar
Sensitive to acid; prefers pH 8.5 (alkaliphilic) and 37¬∞C
ü¶† Virulence & Pathogenicity:
  • Cholera toxin (CT): AB toxin that increases cAMP ‚Üí watery diarrhea
  • B subunit binds GM1 receptors; A subunit activates adenylate cyclase
  • Toxin-coregulated pilus (TCP): aids colonization
  • Accessory toxins: hemolysin, zonula occludens toxin
  • Encoded by CTXœï bacteriophage
üè• Clinical Importance:
  • Causes cholera: profuse "rice-water" stools, dehydration, shock
  • Transmitted via contaminated water, seafood, or food
  • Can lead to death from hypovolemic shock if untreated
  • Managed with oral rehydration salts (ORS) and antibiotics
  • Preventable by sanitation and oral cholera vaccines
