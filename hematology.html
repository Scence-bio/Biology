Platelet Cell
Definition:
Small, colorless, disc-shaped cell fragments in blood
Formed from cytoplasm of megakaryocytes in bone marrow
Not complete cells but play crucial role in blood clotting
Appearance:
Size: Very small (2-4 Œºm in diameter)
Shape: Disc-like when inactive; irregular with finger-like projections when active
Structure: Contains granules storing clotting factors, growth factors, serotonin, calcium

  • Blood clotting: Stick to injury sites (adhesion), clump together (aggregation)

  • Immune role: Interact with white blood cells and pathogens

Basophil Cell (WBC)
Definition:
Type of white blood cell belonging to granulocytes
Plays role in allergic reactions, inflammation, parasite defense
Appearance:
Large cell (10-14 Œºm)
Contains bilobed nucleus
Cytoplasm filled with large, dark blue-purple granules

‚öôÔ∏è Functions:

Slide 5
Eosinophil Cell (WBC)
Definition:
Type of white blood cell belonging to granulocyte group
Important role in defense against parasitic infections
Controls allergic and inflammatory responses
Appearance:
Medium-sized cell (12-17 Œºm)
Bilobed nucleus
Cytoplasm contains large, coarse red-orange granules (eosin staining)
Granules contain enzymes and proteins toxic to parasites
‚öôÔ∏è Functions:
  • Parasitic defense: Releases toxic proteins to kill helminths and parasites

Slide 6
Neutrophil Cell (WBC)
Definition:
Most abundant type of white blood cell
Member of granulocyte group
First line of defense specializing in phagocytosis
Appearance:
Medium-sized cell (10-15 Œºm)
Multilobed nucleus (2-5 lobes) - "polymorphonuclear" appearance
Cytoplasm contains fine, pale neutral-staining granules
‚öôÔ∏è Functions:
  • Phagocytosis: Engulf and digest bacteria, fungi, dead tissue
  • First responders: Rapidly migrate to infection or injury sites
  • Inflammation: Release enzymes and reactive oxygen species
  • Short lifespan: Live few hours to days, forming pus when they die

Appearance:
Largest WBC (15-20 Œºm)

Blood Smear
Definition:
Laboratory test with thin blood layer on microscope slide
Stained and examined under microscope
Used to study blood cell appearance, number, and shape
Appearance:
Thin film of blood on glass slide
Stained with special dyes (Wright's stain, Giemsa stain)
Shows different blood cell types under microscope
‚öôÔ∏è Functions:
  • Diagnostic tool: Detect anemia, infections, leukemia, malaria
  • Cell evaluation: Check size, shape, number of RBCs, WBCs, platelets
  • Parasite detection: Useful in diagnosing malaria and other parasites
  • Differential WBC count: Classify types of white blood cells
Slide 9
Bone Marrow
Definition:
Soft, spongy tissue found inside hollow bone cavities
Primary site of blood cell production (hematopoiesis)
Generates red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets
Appearance:
Soft, gelatinous tissue
Red marrow: reddish-brown (active blood formation)
Yellow marrow: yellowish (mostly fat storage)
Contains hematopoietic stem cells, fat cells, stromal cells
‚öôÔ∏è Functions:
  • Hematopoiesis: Produces all blood cell types
  • Immune function: Site for developing lymphocytes (B cells mature here)
  • Storage: Yellow marrow stores fat as energy reserve
  • Diagnostic importance: Examination helps diagnose blood disorders
Slide 10
Blood Clot Formation
Definition:
Process where blood changes from liquid to gel-like state
Stops bleeding after injury (hemostasis)
Involves platelets, clotting factors, and fibrin
Appearance:
Visible as gel-like mass at injury site
Initially platelet plug, then stabilized by fibrin mesh
In test tube: blood coagulates forming visible clot
‚öôÔ∏è Process & Functions:
  • Vascular spasm: Blood vessels constrict
  • Platelet plug formation: Platelets aggregate at injury
  • Coagulation cascade: Clotting factors activate fibrin formation
  • Fibrinolysis: Clot dissolves after healing
Slide 11
Sickle Cell Anemia
Definition:
Condition where RBCs become abnormally crescent or sickle-shaped
Due to genetic mutation in hemoglobin gene (HbS)
Leads to sickle cell disease (SCD)
Appearance:
RBCs are crescent/sickle-shaped instead of normal biconcave disc
Cells are rigid, less flexible, can clump together
Visible under microscope, especially in oxygen-deprived conditions
‚öôÔ∏è Effects & Complications:
  • Oxygen transport impaired: Sickled cells carry less oxygen, block blood flow
  • Shortened lifespan: 10-20 days (normal RBC ~120 days), leading to anemia
  • Complications: Pain crises, organ damage, infections
  • Genetic importance: Sickle cell trait provides malaria resistance
Slide 12
Multiple Myeloma
Definition:
Cancer of plasma cells (type of white blood cell producing antibodies)
Abnormal plasma cells multiply uncontrollably in bone marrow
Crowds out normal blood cells
Appearance:
Bone marrow contains clusters of malignant plasma cells with large nuclei
Blood shows high levels of abnormal antibodies (M-protein)
Bones show lesions or thinning on X-ray
‚öôÔ∏è Effects & Complications:
  • Antibody production disrupted: Malignant cells produce defective antibodies
  • Bone damage: Causes bone pain, fractures, hypercalcemia
  • Blood cell reduction: Crowding bone marrow leads to anemia, infections, bleeding
  • Kidney damage: Abnormal proteins can damage kidneys
Slide 13
Thalassemia
Definition:
Genetic blood disorder affecting hemoglobin production
Body produces abnormal hemoglobin
Leads to reduced functional red blood cell production and anemia
Appearance:
RBCs are small (microcytic) and pale (hypochromic) under microscope
Cells may appear irregularly shaped and sometimes fragmented
Target cells and other abnormal RBC shapes visible
‚öôÔ∏è Effects & Complications:
  • Impaired oxygen transport: Abnormal hemoglobin reduces oxygen delivery
  • Anemia: Causes fatigue, weakness, pallor
  • Bone changes: Chronic anemia may lead to bone deformities
  • Growth retardation: Severe cases affect growth and development in children
Slide 14
Paroxysmal Nocturnal Hemoglobinuria (PNH)
Definition:
Rare, acquired blood disorder
RBCs destroyed by immune system due to lack of protective surface proteins
Caused by mutation in PIGA gene
Appearance:
RBCs may look normal under microscope but are fragile
Lab tests show hemoglobin in urine (dark-colored urine, especially morning)
Flow cytometry shows absence of CD55 and CD59 proteins
‚öôÔ∏è Effects & Complications:
  • Defective stem cells: Leads to abnormal RBCs, WBCs, and platelets production
  • Hemolysis: RBC destruction causes anemia, dark urine, jaundice
  • Blood clots (thrombosis): Major cause of death in PNH patients
  • Bone marrow failure: Can lead to low counts of all blood cells
Slide 15
Malaria (Blood Stage)
Definition:
Blood stage of malaria where Plasmodium parasites invade RBCs
Parasites multiply inside RBCs and eventually destroy them
Responsible for malaria symptoms
Appearance:
Ring stage: Small ring-like parasite inside RBCs
Trophozoite stage: Larger, more active form
Schizont stage: RBC filled with many daughter parasites (merozoites)
Gametocyte stage: Sexual forms (banana-shaped in P. falciparum)
‚öôÔ∏è Effects & Complications:
  • RBC invasion and multiplication: Each infected RBC releases 8-32 merozoites
  • Hemolysis: RBC rupture causes anemia and releases toxins
  • Cyclic fevers: Symptoms occur in cycles (every 48 or 72 hours)
  • Severe complications: Anemia, enlarged spleen, cerebral malaria, organ failure
Slide 16
Visceral Leishmaniasis (Kala-azar)
Definition:
Parasitic disease caused by Leishmania donovani and related species
Transmitted through bite of infected female sandflies (Phlebotomus species)
Also known as Kala-azar
Appearance:
In blood/bone marrow smears: small, oval parasites (2-5 Œºm)
Found inside macrophages (called amastigotes)
Enlarged spleen and liver visible clinically
‚öôÔ∏è Effects & Complications:
  • Infection of macrophages: Parasite multiplies inside immune cells
  • Spreads through blood, spleen, liver, and bone marrow
  • Complications: Can be fatal if untreated due to severe anemia
  • Secondary infections and organ failure possible
Slide 17
Leukemia (Blood Cancer)
Definition:
Cancer of blood and bone marrow
Causes uncontrolled production of abnormal white blood cells
Abnormal cells don't function properly and crowd out normal cells
Appearance:
Blood smear shows immature or abnormal WBCs (blasts)
Bone marrow often packed with abnormal leukocytes
Normal RBCs and platelets are reduced
‚öôÔ∏è Effects & Complications:
  • Immune dysfunction: Abnormal WBCs cannot fight infections properly
  • Anemia: Reduced RBCs cause fatigue, weakness, pallor
  • Bleeding and bruising: Low platelet count
  • Enlarged organs: Spleen, liver, and lymph nodes may enlarge
Slide 18
Conidium
Definition:
Asexual spore produced by many fungi
Formed externally on specialized hyphae called conidiophores
Does not develop inside a sac (sporangium)
Appearance:
Microscopic, usually round, oval, or elongated
Found singly, in chains, or clusters
May be colorless (hyaline) or pigmented (green, brown, black)
‚öôÔ∏è Functions & Importance:
  • Reproduction: Allows fungi to reproduce asexually
  • Dispersal: Spread easily by air, water, or insects
  • Medical importance: Some cause human infections (e.g., Aspergillus)
  • Agricultural importance: Some cause plant diseases (e.g., powdery mildew)

  • Industrial applications: Used in biotechnology and food production
