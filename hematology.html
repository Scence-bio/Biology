Platelets
Definition
Platelets are small, colorless, disc-shaped cell fragments in the blood, formed from the cytoplasm of megakaryocytes in the bone marrow. They are not complete cells, but play a crucial role in blood clotting (hemostasis).
Appearance
  • Size: Very small (2-4 Œºm in diameter)
  • Shape: Disc-like when inactive; become irregular with finger projections when activated
  • Structure: Contains granules that store clotting factors, growth factors, serotonin, calcium
Functions
  • Allergic response: Releases histamine, which causes blood vessels to dilate and become leaky, producing allergy symptoms
  • Inflammation: Releases chemicals that attract other immune cells to sites of infection or injury
  • Anticoagulation: Heparin prevents blood clotting at the infection site
  • Parasitic defense: Helps in immune responses against parasites (though eosinophils play a bigger role)
  • Medical importance: Increased basophils (basophilia) may indicate allergies, chronic inflammation, or certain leukemias
-title">Functions
  • Blood clotting: When a blood vessel is injured, platelets stick to the site (adhesion), clump together (aggregation), and form a platelet plug
  • Wound healing: Platelets release growth factors that help tissue repair and new blood vessel formation
  • Immune role: Interact with white blood cells and pathogens, contributing to immune defense
Slide 2
White Blood Cells (Leukocytes)
Definition
A white blood cell (WBC), also called a leukocyte, is a type of blood cell that plays a vital role in the body's immune system, defending against infections, foreign substances, and abnormal cells.
Appearance
  • Larger than red blood cells (about 12-20 ¬µm)
  • Have a nucleus (unlike red blood cells)
  • Various subtypes with different morphologies
Functions
  • Defense: Protect the body against pathogens and foreign substances
  • Immune memory: Lymphocytes (T and B cells) recognize antigens for faster response
  • Disease indicator: Abnormal WBC count can signal infections, immune disorders, or blood cancers
Slide 3
Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes)
Definition
A red blood cell (RBC), also called an erythrocyte, is a specialized blood cell responsible for carrying oxygen from the lungs to body tissues and returning carbon dioxide from tissues to the lungs.
Appearance
  • Small, round, and biconcave disc-shaped
  • Red in color due to the presence of hemoglobin
  • No nucleus in mature cells
Functions
  • Oxygen transport: Carry oxygen from lungs to tissues
  • Carbon dioxide transport: Return CO2 from tissues to lungs
  • pH balance: Help maintain blood pH
  • Survival role: Essential for tissue respiration and energy production
Slide 5
Eosinophils
Definition
An eosinophil is a type of white blood cell (WBC) that belongs to the granulocyte group. It plays an important role in the body's defense against parasitic infections and in controlling allergic and inflammatory responses.
Appearance
  • Medium-sized cell (12-17 ¬µm)
  • Bilobed nucleus
  • Cytoplasm contains large, coarse red-orange granules when stained with eosin dye
  • Granules contain enzymes and proteins (like major basic protein) toxic to parasites
Functions
  • Parasitic defense: Releases toxic proteins to kill helminths (worms) and other parasites
  • Allergy regulation: Modulates allergic responses by breaking down histamine and inflammatory mediators
  • Inflammation: Involved in asthma and allergic diseases (high eosinophil counts are common in these conditions)
  • Immune balance: Helps regulate immune responses by interacting with T cells and mast cells
  • Medical importance: High levels (eosinophilia) may indicate parasitic infections, asthma, hay fever, or allergic disorders
Slide 6
Neutrophils
Definition
A neutrophil is the most abundant type of white blood cell (WBC) and a member of the granulocyte group. It is a first line of defense in the immune system, specializing in phagocytosis (engulfing and destroying bacteria, fungi, and cellular debris).
Appearance
  • Medium-sized cell (10-15 ¬µm)
  • Multilobed nucleus (2-5 lobes, giving a "polymorphonuclear" appearance)
  • Cytoplasm contains fine, pale neutral-staining granules (not strongly colored by acidic or basic dyes)
Functions
  • Phagocytosis: Engulf and digest bacteria, fungi, and dead tissue
  • First responders: Rapidly migrate to sites of infection or injury
  • Inflammation: Release enzymes and reactive oxygen species to kill pathogens
  • Short life span: Live only a few hours to days, then die, forming pus at infection sites
Slide 7
Monocytes
Definition
A monocyte is a large type of white blood cell (WBC) that belongs to the agranulocyte group. It circulates in the blood and migrates into tissues, where it differentiates into macrophages or dendritic cells, playing a central role in immunity.
Appearance
  • Largest WBC (15-20 ¬µm)
  • Kidney-shaped or horseshoe-shaped nucleus
  • Abundant cytoplasm, grayish-blue in color
  • Few or no visible granules (agranular)
Functions
  • Phagocytosis: Engulf pathogens, dead cells, and debris
  • Antigen presentation: Process and present antigens to T cells
  • Cytokine production: Release signaling molecules for immune responses
  • Medical importance: Increased monocytes (monocytosis) may indicate chronic infections (e.g., tuberculosis), inflammation, or blood disorders
Slide 8
Plasma
Definition
Plasma is the liquid portion of blood, making up about 55% of total blood volume. It is a pale yellow fluid composed mostly of water, proteins, electrolytes, hormones, nutrients, and waste products.
Appearance
  • Clear, pale yellow fluid
  • Accounts for more than half of the blood's total volume
  • When blood cells (RBCs, WBCs, and platelets) are removed, plasma remains as the fluid component
Functions
  • Transport: Carries nutrients, hormones, and proteins throughout the body
  • Waste removal: Transports metabolic wastes to excretory organs
  • Clotting: Contains clotting factors (fibrinogen, prothrombin)
  • Immunity: Carries antibodies (immunoglobulins) that fight infections
  • pH & temperature regulation: Maintains blood pH and body temperature
  • Medical importance: Plasma is used in transfusions, production of medicines (like clotting factors, albumin)
Slide 9
Serum
Definition
Serum is the clear liquid part of blood that remains after clotting has occurred. It is similar to plasma but lacks clotting factors (such as fibrinogen and prothrombin), since they are used up in clot formation.
Appearance
  • Clear, pale yellow fluid
  • Obtained by allowing blood to clot, then centrifuging to remove the clot and blood cells
  • Slightly less protein content compared to plasma (because clotting proteins are removed)
Functions
  • Transport: Contains electrolytes, hormones, antibodies, and nutrients for transport
  • Immunity: Rich in antibodies (immunoglobulins) that help fight infections
  • Diagnostics: Widely used in medical tests (like hormone levels, antibodies, infections, and biochemistry tests)
  • Research & vaccines: Serum is important for producing antiserum (used against toxins, venoms, or infections)
  • Medical difference: Plasma = with clotting factors; Serum = without clotting factors
Slide 10
Blood Smear
Definition
A blood smear (or peripheral blood smear) is a laboratory test in which a thin layer of blood is spread on a microscope slide, stained, and examined under a microscope. It is used to study the appearance, number, and shape of blood cells.
Appearance
  • Appears as a thin film of blood on a glass slide
  • Stained with special dyes (e.g., Wright's stain, Giemsa stain)
  • Shows individual blood cells under the microscope
Functions
  • Diagnostic tool: Helps detect anemia, infections, leukemia, malaria, thalassemia, and others
  • Cell evaluation: Used to check size, shape, and number of RBCs, WBCs, and platelets
  • Parasite detection: Useful in diagnosing malaria and other parasitic infections
  • Differential WBC count: Helps classify types of WBCs
  • Medical importance: A quick, inexpensive, and reliable method to identify abnormalities in blood
Slide 11
Bone Marrow
Definition
Bone marrow is the soft, spongy tissue found inside the hollow cavities of bones. It is the primary site of blood cell production (hematopoiesis), generating red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets.
Appearance
  • Soft, gelatinous tissue with a reddish-brown color (red marrow) or yellowish color (yellow marrow, mostly fat)
  • Composed of hematopoietic stem cells, fat cells, and supporting stromal cells
  • Red marrow is active in blood formation; yellow marrow mainly stores fat
Functions
  • Hematopoiesis: Produces all blood cell types (RBCs, WBCs, platelets)
  • Immune function: Provides a site for developing lymphocytes (B cells mature here)
  • Storage: Yellow marrow stores fat, which can serve as energy reserve
  • Medical importance: Bone marrow examination helps diagnose anemia, leukemia, lymphoma, myeloma, and other blood disorders
Slide 12
Blood Clot Formation
Definition
Blood clot formation is the process by which blood changes from a liquid to a gel-like state to stop bleeding after an injury. This process is also called hemostasis and involves platelets, clotting factors, and fibrin.
Appearance
  • Visible as a gel-like mass at the site of injury
  • Initially a platelet plug forms, then it is stabilized by a fibrin mesh
  • In vitro (test tube), appears as a solid clot separated from serum
Functions & Process
  • Stop bleeding: Primary function to prevent blood loss
  • Wound repair: Provides framework for tissue healing
  • Protection: Prevents entry of pathogens through wounds
  • Vascular spasm: Blood vessels constrict
  • Platelet plug formation: Platelets aggregate at injury site
  • Fibrin clot formation: Coagulation cascade produces fibrin mesh
  • Fibrinolysis: Clot is dissolved after healing
Slide 13
Sickle Cell Disease
Definition
Sickle cell refers to a condition in which red blood cells (RBCs) become abnormally shaped like a crescent or sickle. This occurs due to a genetic mutation in the hemoglobin gene (HbS), leading to sickle cell disease (SCD).
Appearance
  • RBCs are crescent- or sickle-shaped instead of the normal biconcave disc
  • Cells are rigid, less flexible, and can clump together
  • Visible under a microscope, especially in oxygen-deprived conditions
Effects & Complications
  • Oxygen transport impaired: Sickled cells carry less oxygen and can block blood flow
  • Shortened lifespan: Lifespan of sickle cells is ~10-20 days (normal RBC ~120 days), leading to anemia
  • Complications: Can cause pain crises, organ damage, infections
  • Genetic importance: Sickle cell trait (heterozygous) provides some resistance to malaria
  • Medical importance: Diagnosis involves blood smear, hemoglobin electrophoresis, and genetic testing
Slide 14
Multiple Myeloma
Definition
Multiple myeloma is a cancer of plasma cells, a type of white blood cell that produces antibodies. In this disease, abnormal plasma cells multiply uncontrollably in the bone marrow, crowding out normal blood cells.
Appearance
  • Bone marrow contains clusters of malignant plasma cells with large nuclei
  • Blood may show high levels of abnormal antibodies (M-protein)
  • Bones may show lesions or thinning on X-ray due to plasma cell infiltration
Effects & Complications
  • Antibody production disrupted: Malignant plasma cells produce defective antibodies
  • Bone damage: Causes bone pain, fractures, and hypercalcemia
  • Blood cell reduction: Crowding bone marrow leads to anemia, thrombocytopenia, leukopenia
  • Kidney damage: Abnormal proteins can damage kidney function
  • Medical importance: Diagnosis involves blood tests (M-protein), urine tests, bone marrow biopsy, and imaging
Slide 15
Thalassemia
Definition
Thalassemia is a genetic blood disorder in which the body produces abnormal hemoglobin, leading to reduced production of functional red blood cells and resulting in anemia.
Appearance
  • Red blood cells are small (microcytic) and pale (hypochromic) under a microscope
  • Cells may appear irregularly shaped and sometimes fragmented
  • Target cells may be present
Effects & Treatment
  • Impaired oxygen transport: Abnormal hemoglobin reduces oxygen delivery to tissues
  • Anemia: Causes fatigue, weakness, and pallor
  • Bone changes: Chronic anemia may lead to bone deformities (especially in the face and skull)
  • Growth retardation: Severe thalassemia can affect growth and development in children
  • Diagnosis: Blood tests, hemoglobin electrophoresis, and genetic testing
  • Treatment: Blood transfusions, iron chelation therapy, and sometimes bone marrow transplantation
Slide 16
Paroxysmal Nocturnal Hemoglobinuria (PNH)
Definition
Paroxysmal Nocturnal Hemoglobinuria (PNH) is a rare, acquired blood disorder where red blood cells (RBCs) are destroyed by the immune system because they lack protective surface proteins due to a mutation in the PIGA gene.
Appearance
  • Under a microscope, RBCs may look normal but are fragile and easily destroyed (hemolysis)
  • Lab tests show hemoglobin in urine (dark-colored urine, especially in the morning)
  • Flow cytometry shows absence of CD55 and CD59 proteins on blood cells
Effects & Complications
  • Defective stem cells: Leads to production of abnormal RBCs, WBCs, and platelets
  • Hemolysis (RBC destruction): Causes anemia, dark urine, and jaundice
  • Blood clots (thrombosis): A major cause of death in PNH patients
  • Bone marrow failure: Can lead to low counts of all blood cells (pancytopenia)
Slide 17
Malaria (Blood Stage)
Definition
When malaria parasites (Plasmodium species) enter the blood stage, they invade red blood cells (RBCs), multiply inside them, and eventually destroy them. This blood stage is responsible for the symptoms of malaria.
Appearance
  • Ring stage (early trophozoite): Small ring-like parasite inside RBCs
  • Trophozoite stage: Larger, more active form
  • Schizont stage: RBC filled with many daughter parasites (merozoites)
  • Gametocyte stage: Sexual forms (banana-shaped in P. falciparum)
  • Infected RBCs may look enlarged, deformed, or ruptured depending on the species
Process & Complications
  • RBC invasion: Parasite enters RBCs using special proteins
  • Multiplication: Each infected RBC can release 8-32 merozoites after rupture
  • Hemolysis: Rupture of RBCs causes anemia and releases toxins, leading to fever and chills
  • Cyclic fevers: Symptoms occur in cycles (every 48 or 72 hours) depending on parasite species
  • Severe anemia and enlarged spleen
  • Cerebral malaria: parasite-infected RBCs block brain vessels
  • Multi-organ failure in severe P. falciparum infections
Slide 18
Kala-azar (Visceral Leishmaniasis)
Definition
Kala-azar (Visceral leishmaniasis) is a parasitic disease caused by Leishmania donovani and related species. It is transmitted to humans through the bite of infected female sandflies (Phlebotomus species).
Appearance
  • In blood or bone marrow smears: small, oval parasites (2-5 ¬µm) inside macrophages (called amastigotes)
  • Enlarged spleen and liver in patients (clinically visible)
  • Parasites appear as Leishman-Donovan bodies within infected cells
Effects & Complications
  • Infection of macrophages: The parasite multiplies inside immune cells, spreading through blood, spleen, liver, and bone marrow
  • Fever, weight loss, and weakness
  • Hepatosplenomegaly: Enlarged liver and spleen
  • Anemia and reduced white blood cell count
  • Complications: Can be fatal if untreated due to severe anemia, infections, and organ failure
Slide 19
Leukemia
Definition
Leukemia is a cancer of the blood and bone marrow that causes uncontrolled production of abnormal white blood cells (WBCs). These cells do not function properly and crowd out normal blood cells.
Appearance
  • Blood smear shows immature or abnormal WBCs (called blasts)
  • Bone marrow is often packed with abnormal leukocytes
  • Normal RBCs and platelets are reduced
  • Abnormal cell morphology and increased cell count
Effects & Complications
  • Immune dysfunction: Abnormal WBCs cannot fight infections effectively
  • Anemia: Due to reduced RBC production ‚Üí fatigue, weakness, pallor
  • Bleeding & bruising: Low platelet count leads to bleeding disorders
  • Enlarged organs: Spleen, liver, and lymph nodes may become enlarged
  • Bone pain and weakness
  • Increased susceptibility to infections
Slide 4
Basophils
Definition
A basophil is a type of white blood cell (WBC) that belongs to the group of granulocytes. It plays a role in allergic reactions, inflammation, and defense against parasites by releasing chemical mediators such as histamine and heparin.
Appearance
  • Large cell (10-14 ¬µm)
  • Contains a bilobed nucleus
  • Cytoplasm filled with large, dark blue-purple granules
  • Granules contain histamine, heparin, and other mediators
